About the System

A mobile application enables users to capture or upload archaeological objects, which are then processed through an AI-powered pipeline. The system begins by extracting features from the object to understand its visual and structural elements. These features are passed to Gemini AI, which generates intelligent prompts that guide the creation of digital representations. Depending on the desired output, the prompts are used by Imagine AI to produce detailed 2D images or by Meshy AI to construct interactive 3D models. The final result, whether a 2D visual or a 3D reconstruction, is displayed within the app, providing users with an enriched, digitally-enhanced interpretation of the original object.

Stone Tools

Studying stone tools helps us learn about early human life, technology, and culture. Researchers often analyze these tools using simple methods, but advanced technologies like AI and smart algorithms are not widely used yet. This leaves a gap in making the analysis of stone tools more detailed and accurate.

Pottery

Pottery items are among the most known archeological objects that present direct cultural information and historical significances to different eras. Their architecture can reveal valuable information about ancient technologies. However, these archeological objects can also be represented digitally and preserved for future generations using modern technology like digital image processing and AI.

Bones Artifacts

What Do I Do? Capture and analyze bone artifact images. Extract detailed features like carvings and wear patterns. Create a model to generate precise data for artifact study. Why Do I Do This? Traditional methods are slow and error-prone. Advanced technology can improve accuracy and efficiency. To preserve and make cultural heritage accessible for research and education.

Coines

Studying archaeological coins helps us understand the information about past economic systems, and cultural influences. Normally people use physical methods to analyze these coins. So, These coins can also be represented digitally and preserved for future generations using modern technology like digital image processing, 3D visualization and AI tools.

LITERATURE SURVEY

Recent advancements in artificial intelligence (AI) and mobile computing have significantly influenced the field of digital archaeology. Traditional methods for documenting and analyzing artifacts—such as manual sketching, typological classification, and lab-based imaging—are often time-consuming, expensive, and inaccessible to the general public. The integration of mobile applications with AI offers a scalable solution for real-time artifact analysis, bringing archaeological research to broader audiences. Systems like Google Arts & Culture and the Venice Time Machine demonstrate the potential of AI in cultural documentation, yet they are often not optimized for mobile-first, field-based use. Furthermore, the use of generative AI tools like Imagine AI for 2D image restoration and Meshy AI for 3D reconstruction is gaining traction in virtual heritage visualization, making the integration of such tools into field-deployable platforms a growing area of interest..

RESEARCH PROBLEM

Despite the emergence of digital tools for heritage preservation, there remains a significant gap in creating an integrated, mobile-first platform that can scan, interpret, and visually reconstruct archaeological objects in real-time. Existing solutions tend to be fragmented, lack on-device AI processing capabilities, or rely heavily on controlled lab environments. Many of these systems also overlook contextual user engagement, multilingual prompt generation, and category-specific artifact understanding, which are essential for democratizing archaeological documentation. There is a need for a system that enables users—including students, researchers, and the public—to accurately analyze and digitally reconstruct artifacts using only a smartphone.

RESEARCH GAP

While current literature and technologies demonstrate isolated use cases of AI in artifact classification or image generation, most platforms fail to offer a unified, extensible framework that supports multi-modal outputs. Key limitations include reliance on cloud-only models, absence of real-time mobile processing, lack of modular design for various artifact types, and minimal support for interactive 3D visualization. Moreover, advanced technologies like MobileNet, ResNet, or NLP-based prompt generation—while explored in academic contexts—often require additional training data or infrastructure, making them less practical for real-world mobile deployments. Therefore, a gap exists in developing a system that is lightweight, category-aware, and deployable on common mobile hardware using real-time AI integration.

MAIN OBJECTIVE

The mobile application is designed to digitally capture, analyze, and visually reconstruct archaeological objects using artificial intelligence, enabling real-time interpretation through a smartphone. It aims to democratize access to cultural heritage by providing both experts and the general public with a tool that converts physical artifacts into detailed 2D and 3D digital representations. By integrating AI-driven prompt generation and generative visualization technologies, the system bridges the gap between archaeological discovery and educational engagement.

Specific Objectives

The system processes images captured through the mobile app and extracts critical visual features such as shape, texture, engravings, and physical structure using computer vision techniques. These features allow for accurate identification and categorization of artifacts, supporting real-time field-based classification without requiring lab-grade equipment.

Semantic Prompt Generation Using Gemini AI
Based on the extracted visual data, the system utilizes Gemini AI to generate semantically rich prompts. These descriptive prompts form the foundation for creating historically meaningful interpretations and guide the downstream generation of 2D and 3D outputs. This enables non-experts to engage with artifacts through accessible narratives.

Real-Time 2D Image Reconstruction via Imagine AI
TImagine AI generates high-fidelity, artistically enhanced 2D representations of the scanned artifacts using the AI-generated prompts. This feature supports use cases such as restoration previews, educational visuals, and artifact presentation for museums or field research.

Immersive 3D Model Generation with Meshy AI
The system uses Meshy AI to generate interactive 3D mesh models from extracted features and prompts. These models allow users to rotate, zoom, and view artifacts from multiple angles, enhancing the learning experience and making digital archaeology immersive and accessible.

Modular Design for Multi-Class Artifact Support
Each artifact category—such as tools, coins, bones, and pottery—is handled through dedicated modules that share a common architecture but are tailored to domain-specific features. This modular approach ensures high recognition accuracy while allowing scalability for future artifact classes.

Offline Functionality and Field Usability
To support field archaeologists and researchers in low-connectivity environments, the system includes local image processing, temporary storage, and model inference capabilities using lightweight .tflite models, ensuring that the core functions operate without reliance on continuous internet access.

User-Centric Visualization and Export Tools
The mobile app presents users with intuitive visual outputs and allows easy sharing, export, or cloud sync of results. 2D and 3D results, along with AI-generated descriptions, can be saved locally or uploaded to Firebase and Google Cloud for collaborative usage or archival purposes.

Support for Educational and Public Engagement
By enabling artifact interaction through descriptive prompts and visual reconstructions, the system supports educational institutions, museums, and cultural organizations in promoting archaeological learning. It lowers the barrier for participation by offering a simple mobile-based interface usable by students and history enthusiasts.

METHODOLOGY

This project adopts a phased, mixed-method approach consisting of both qualitative and quantitative research strategies. Initial requirements were gathered through consultations with archaeologists, museum staff, and educators to define artifact categories and user needs. The system design phase focused on usability in low-connectivity environments, modularity, and category-specific AI modeling. Feature extraction was implemented using OpenCV, while backend services were developed with Python and Flask. Gemini AI was used to generate prompts from extracted features, guiding output generation via Imagine AI (2D) and Meshy AI (3D). The final implementation was tested through expert validation, student usability trials, and field pilot deployments to ensure reliability, interpretability, and user satisfaction.
